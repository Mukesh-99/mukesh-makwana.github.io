About Me

I am Mukesh Makwana, a cognitive scientist with a passion for understanding how humans perceive time, experience agency, and make decisions in dynamic environments. My research spans multiple domains including perception, attention, time perception, action, and agency, with applications in human-machine interaction, rehabilitation, education, and the ethics of artificial intelligence.

As a first-generation student who has traveled over 10,600 miles across the globe for my education, I bring a unique perspective to cognitive science. My journey from India to the United States has enriched my understanding of diverse perspectives and the importance of inclusive research and teaching.

Currently, I am a Research Affiliate in the Donoghue lab at Brown University, where I investigate neural decoding of complex reaching and grasping actions, sense-of-agency in individuals with tetraplegia using intracranial-BCI, and explore the neural signature of subconscious perception in the motor cortex.

As an educator and mentor, I am committed to fostering inclusive learning environments where students from diverse backgrounds feel welcomed, valued, and supported. I believe that embracing diversity is essential for scientific progress and innovation.

Research

My research program examines fundamental questions about human cognition, with a focus on how we perceive time, experience agency, and make decisions in dynamic environments. I use behavioral psychophysics, action and eye tracking, computational modeling, and neural decoding to advance our understanding of these processes.

Time Perception

My doctoral research demonstrated that intention systematically alters time perception. Intended outcomes are experienced as longer in duration than unintended ones, reflecting changes in the internal clock model. I proposed the Onset Shifting Model to account for these effects, suggesting that intention pre-activates outcome representations.

Agency and the Self

I investigate how we distinguish our own actions from those of others. My work with meditation researchers shows that mindfulness meditation reduces the self-other boundary, while my collaboration with the BrainGate group examines agency in patients using brain-computer interfaces.

Attention and Action

My postdoctoral research focused on selection history - how past choices influence present actions. I showed that reaching trajectories are shaped by both inhibition from previous distractors and activation of prior targets, revealing the dynamic nature of attention.

Teaching

Teaching Philosophy

I approach teaching through three core principles grounded in human learning:

  • Storytelling: Narratives make complex concepts concrete and memorable through thought experiments and case studies
  • Active Engagement: Games, role-play, and interactive demonstrations foster motivation and participation
  • Problem-Solving: Real-world problems challenge students to apply knowledge and develop critical thinking skills

Underlying all three is a commitment to inclusion, ensuring that students from diverse neurodivergent, cultural, linguistic, and socioeconomic backgrounds feel seen, respected, and supported.
